BJW-RiskLab Reference Manual

BJW-RiskLab is a closed-form blackjack risk calculator. It answers the quantitative questions advantage players need before, during, and after a session — bankroll sizing, ruin probability, session variance, goal probability, and bet optimisation — all derived analytically from published formulas, with no Monte Carlo simulation.

The primary formula source is Don Schlesinger’s Blackjack Attack: Playing the Pros’ Way, 3rd edition (BJA3), Chapter 10. This is the same source used by CVCX (Norman Wattenberger) and the original Blackjack Risk Manager (BJRM, John Auston, 1998). The standard normal CDF is implemented via Abramowitz & Stegun (1964) formula 26.2.17.

How the tabs connect

The four calculator tabs share common state: μ₁₀₀ (win rate per 100 hands) and σ₁₀₀ (SD per 100 hands) flow through every calculation. You can type these directly in the shared input bar, or load them automatically by clicking a row in Tab 3 (Game Library) or running Tab 4 (Bet Optimizer). Tab 4 propagates its results back to Tabs 1 and 2 instantly.

Unit convention: All risk calculations work in units — multiples of your minimum bet. Dollar figures are derived by multiplying unit values by the “Unit size ($)” field. This convention matches BJA3 and CVCX exactly.

Shared Inputs

These four fields sit in the bar below the header and apply to every tab simultaneously. Changing any one of them triggers an instant recalculation across all tabs.

Tab 1 Risk of Ruin & Bankroll

Tab 1 answers the foundational bankroll questions: given your edge and bankroll size, what is the probability of going broke? How large does your bankroll need to be for a given safety target? How long until your edge overcomes luck? How much do you earn per hour?

Tab 1 inputs

FieldSymbolDescription
Bankroll (units)BTotal units dedicated to blackjack — your full playing bankroll, not a trip stake. Using a trip stake instead will significantly overstate RoR.
Target RoRRoR*Maximum ruin probability you will accept, as a decimal (e.g. 0.01 = 1%). Used only for the Required Bankroll output — does not affect the RoR calculation for bankroll B.

Risk of Ruin (infinite play)

The probability that your bankroll will eventually reach zero if you play indefinitely with no quit point.

RoR = exp( −2 × μ₁₀₀ × B / σ₁₀₀² )

where:
  μ₁₀₀ = win rate per 100 hands (units)
  σ₁₀₀ = standard deviation per 100 hands (units)
  B    = bankroll (units)
  exp() = natural exponential function

Source: BJA3 p.112. Per-hand and per-100-hand values cancel exactly in this formula.

The formula assumes infinite play time and no quit point. Because real players do stop, this is a conservative overestimate for any real scenario.

Risk level colour coding

≤ 1%  Professional standard (≈ quarter Kelly)
1–5%  Acceptable for most players
5–13.5%  Aggressive; approaches full Kelly
>13.5%  Over-Kelly; bankroll too small for unit size

Required Bankroll

The RoR formula rearranged to find bankroll B given a target RoR:

B = −ln(RoR*) × σ₁₀₀² / ( 2 × μ₁₀₀ )

where:
  RoR* = target risk of ruin (e.g. 0.01 for 1%)
  ln()  = natural logarithm

Source: BJA3 p.112, rearranged. Exact algebraic inverse of the RoR formula.

Common benchmarks: 1% RoR requires roughly 460 units for a typical 6D game (μ=0.80, σ=14). 13.5% RoR (full Kelly) requires roughly 245 units for the same game — exactly half the 1% bankroll.

N₀ — Hands to Overcome Variance

N₀ (N-naught) is the number of hands at which your cumulative expected profit equals one standard deviation of your cumulative results. It marks the threshold where skill begins to statistically dominate luck.

N₀ = ( σ₁₀₀ / μ₁₀₀ )² × 100

Units: hands.
Before N₀ hands: variance dominates; results are mostly noise.
After  N₀ hands: EV dominates; your edge becomes statistically visible.

Source: BJA3 Chapter 10. Lower N₀ means the edge overcomes luck sooner — a better game.

A typical 6-deck counter with a 1–12 spread at 75% penetration has N₀ around 25,000–35,000 hands — roughly 300–450 hours at 80 HPH. This is why short-run results tell you almost nothing about whether you are playing well.

Dollar Rates

Hourly EV ($) = ( μ₁₀₀ / 100 ) × HPH × u

Hourly SD ($) = σ₁₀₀ × √( HPH / 100 ) × u

where u = unit size in dollars, HPH = hands per hour

EV scales linearly with time; SD scales with the square root. Longer sessions have better signal-to-noise ratios. About 68% of one-hour sessions fall within ±1 SD of the EV; about 95% within ±2 SD.

Tab 2 Session / Trip Analysis

Tab 2 analyses a finite playing session. It answers: “What is the realistic range of outcomes for my 10-hour trip?”, “How likely am I to hit my stop-loss?”, and “Was my result unusually bad or just normal variance?”

Tab 2 inputs

FieldSymbolDescription
Hours of playHSession duration. Multiplied by HPH to get total hands N = H × HPH.
Stop-loss (units)SAmount you are willing to lose before ending the session. Enter as a positive number.
Win goal (units)GProfit target at which you would stop playing. Enter as a positive number.
Confidence level—Width of the result range interval. 95% means 95% of session outcomes fall within the displayed bounds. Choose 95.45% for exact ±2σ, or 68.27% for ±1σ.
Actual result (units)ROptional. Enter your actual result (positive if won, negative if lost) to compute how unusual it was. Leave blank to skip.

Session Statistics

For a session of N = H × HPH hands:

Mean result (units) = μ₁₀₀ × ( N / 100 )
SD of result (units) = σ₁₀₀ × √( N / 100 )

Mean result ($) = Mean result (units) × u
SD of result ($) = SD of result (units) × u

Source: Central Limit Theorem applied to i.i.d. hand outcomes; BJA3 Chapter 10.

Result Range (Confidence Interval)

Given confidence level C, the interval [lo, hi] contains (C × 100)% of all possible N-hand session outcomes:

z  = Φ¹( 0.5 + C/2 )    (inverse normal CDF)
lo = Mean − z × SD
hi = Mean + z × SD

Examples:
  C = 0.95   → z = 1.9600 → 95% of outcomes fall in [lo, hi]
  C = 0.9545 → z = 2.0000 → exactly ±2σ band
  C = 0.6827 → z = 1.0000 → exactly ±1σ band
Important: The result range is centred on your expected profit, not on zero. A counter with a positive edge will have an interval that is asymmetrically shifted upward. Even the lower bound may be positive for long sessions with strong edges.

Result Rarity

Enter your actual trip result R to compute how unusual it was relative to expectation.

z = ( R − Mean ) / SD

P(result ≤ R) = Φ(z)    "this bad or worse"
P(result ≥ R) = 1 − Φ(z)  "this good or better"

If R < Mean: displays P(result ≤ R)
If R ≥ Mean: displays P(result ≥ R)

A value below 5% indicates the result was in the left tail of the distribution — bad variance, not necessarily bad play.

Trip Risk of Ruin

The probability of hitting your stop-loss during a finite session of N hands. Always lower than infinite-play RoR because you eventually stop playing.

Trip RoR ≈ Φ(z₁) + exp( −2μS / σ²_per_hand ) × Φ(z₂)

where:
  z₁ = ( −S − μN ) / σN
  z₂ = ( −S + μN ) / σN
  μN = μ₁₀₀ × (N/100)    (cumulative mean)
  σN = σ₁₀₀ × √(N/100)  (cumulative SD)
  σ²_per_hand = σ₁₀₀² / 100
  S = stop-loss in units (positive value)

Source: BJA3 p.132, “The Short-Term, or Trip, Ruin Formula.”

Known overestimate: Schlesinger and Wattenberger both document that this formula slightly overestimates trip RoR compared to simulation. CVCX produces the same formula result, so BJW-RiskLab and CVCX will agree; both are marginally conservative relative to a full Monte Carlo result.

Goal Probabilities

P(reach goal | no time limit) — BJA3 p.138–139

Probability of reaching win goal G before losing stop-loss S, with unlimited time:

P = ( 1 − exp(−α_S) ) / ( 1 − exp(−α_(S+G)) )

where:
  α_X = 2 × μ₁₀₀ × X / σ₁₀₀²

Special case: if μ₁₀₀ = 0 (fair game), P = S / (S + G)

P(reach goal | within session) — BJA3 p.136–137

P_time ≈ P(reach G in N hands) × P(survive session)

where:
  P(reach G in N hands) = 1 − Φ( (G − μN) / σN )
  P(survive session)   = 1 − Trip RoR

This is an approximation. The exact formula (BJA3 p.137) requires an
infinite series. Adequate for most practical inputs.

Tab 3 Game Library

A searchable, sortable reference table of pre-computed μ₁₀₀ and σ₁₀₀ values for standard blackjack scenarios, mirroring BJA3 Chapter 10. Each scenario was simulated for 400 million or more rounds using a Hi-Lo counter with Illustrious 18 and Fab 4 strategy departures.

Click any row to load that scenario’s μ₁₀₀ and σ₁₀₀ into the shared inputs and instantly recalculate all tabs. Click any column header to sort.

Data status: Library values are currently approximate placeholders (marked *). Before using them for real bankroll decisions, verify against the physical BJA3 Chapter 10 tables.

SCORE Column

SCORE is computed live and updates when you change HPH. Default sort is SCORE descending — best games first. Sorting by penetration or spread shows clearly how those variables drive game quality.

Tab 4 Bet Optimizer

Tab 4 computes the optimal bet at each true count level given a game configuration and spread constraint. It outputs μ₁₀₀, σ₁₀₀, SCORE, and Desirability Index, then propagates μ/σ to Tabs 1 and 2.

Inputs

Late SurrenderWhether the player may surrender after seeing dealer upcard. Saves roughly 0.08%.

SCORE and Desirability Index

SCORE = μ₁₀₀² / σ₁₀₀² × 100

Typical ranges:
  0.05–0.15  poor game (8D, low pen, tight spread)
  0.15–0.35  average 6D game
  0.35–0.60  good 6D or average 2D game
  0.60–1.00  excellent game (2D high pen, or wonging)
  >1.00       outstanding (1D or deep-pen 2D)

Source: BJA3 Chapter 10. SCORE is equivalent to the Sharpe ratio in finance.

DI = SCORE × HPH / 100

A mediocre-SCORE game at 200 HPH (heads-up) can produce more dollars
per hour than a high-SCORE game at 60 HPH (full table).

Source: BJA3 Chapter 10.

Kelly Criterion and Bet Sizing

b_Kelly(TC) = edge(TC) / σ²_per_unit × bankroll_units

edge(TC) = base_edge + TC × edge_per_TC_point

Edge-per-TC values by counting system:
  Hi-Lo: ≈ 0.50% per TC point  (BJA3 Chapter 4)
  K-O:  ≈ 0.48% per TC point  (Vancura & Fuchs)
  UBZII: ≈ 0.45% per TC point  (Uston)

Source: Kelly (1956); BJA3 Chapter 4 for edge-per-TC values.

TC frequency approximation: The optimizer uses a Gaussian model for the true count distribution. For production-quality results, replace with pre-computed TC frequency tables from Griffin’s Theory of Blackjack or CVCX simulation data. See VALIDATION_CVCX.md in the repository.

Glossary

μ₁₀₀ — Win Rate per 100 Hands
Units: betting units. Also written mu100.

Mean profit per 100 hands played, in units. The fundamental measure of a counter’s edge in a given game and betting configuration.

σ₁₀₀ — Standard Deviation per 100 Hands
Units: betting units. Also written sigma100.

Standard deviation of results per 100 hands in units. Typical range: 10–13 for flat betting; 13–20 for spread betting.

Risk of Ruin (RoR)
Also: probability of ruin, Gambler’s Ruin probability.

The probability that cumulative results will eventually reach −B (lose the entire bankroll). For a player with positive edge (μ > 0), this is less than 1.0 — but it is never zero. BJA3 p.112.

N₀ (N-naught)
Units: hands. Divide by HPH to convert to hours.

The number of hands at which cumulative EV equals one SD of cumulative results. Before N₀, luck dominates; after N₀, skill becomes statistically significant. BJA3 Chapter 10.

SCORE
Dimensionless. Stands for Standardised Comparison Of Risk and Edge.

Game quality metric: SCORE = (μ₁₀₀ / σ₁₀₀)² × 100. Analogous to the Sharpe ratio in finance. Higher is always better. BJA3 Chapter 10.

Desirability Index (DI)
DI = SCORE × HPH / 100.

SCORE adjusted for table speed. A mediocre-SCORE game played heads-up at 200 HPH may produce more dollars per hour than a high-SCORE crowded game at 60 HPH. BJA3 Chapter 10.

True Count (TC)
Dimensionless integer.

Running count divided by decks remaining to be dealt. Normalises the running count across different penetration levels. For Hi-Lo, each +1 TC point is worth approximately 0.50% in player edge (BJA3 Chapter 4).

Penetration
Expressed as % of shoe dealt.

Proportion of the shoe dealt before reshuffling. Higher penetration = more cards seen = more TC variance = more high-count opportunities. The single most important game condition after deck count.

Wong-In / Back-Counting
Named for Stanford Wong, Professional Blackjack.

A strategy where the counter watches a table without betting until the count reaches a positive threshold, then sits down. Dramatically increases μ₁₀₀ at the cost of higher σ₁₀₀ and reduced HPH. Some casinos prohibit mid-shoe entry to counter this.

Kelly Criterion
Source: Kelly (1956), Bell System Technical Journal.

The formula for the fraction of bankroll to bet that maximises long-run bankroll growth. Full Kelly = edge ÷ variance per unit, producing 13.5% RoR. Half Kelly produces roughly 1.7% RoR and is commonly used by professional players.

Hi-Lo Count
Balanced Level 1 counting system.

The most widely used card counting system. Cards 2–6: +1. Cards 7–9: 0. Cards 10–A: −1. Running count ÷ decks remaining = true count. Hi-Lo is the reference system for all BJA3 Chapter 10 simulations.

Standard Normal CDF Φ(z)
Implemented via Abramowitz & Stegun formula 26.2.17.

Φ(z) = probability that a standard normal variable is ≤ z. Used for result range, rarity, and trip RoR calculations. Maximum absolute error 7.5 × 10&sup8;.
